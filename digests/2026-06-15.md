## Engineering & AI Workflows
- [The Coin Flip Judge? Reliability and Bias in LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13685) — Rigorous study across 29 tasks finds pairwise LLM judge preferences flip 13.6% of the time (one question hit 56%), GPT-4o-mini shows 72% first-position bias, and cross-judge agreement is only 76%. If you're using LLM-as-a-Judge to evaluate AI-generated code quality, single-trial evaluation is too noisy — the paper recommends 11+ repeated trials with position randomization as minimum practice.
- [Why AI hasn't replaced software engineers, and won't](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/14/why-ai-hasnt-replaced-software-engineers/#atom-everything) — Narayanan and Kapoor's analysis identifies the three real bottlenecks AI can't automate away: deciding what to build, being accountable for what ships, and deep contextual understanding of codebase and business. Directly validates the "steering AI output" role over "writing code" — your job is the hard part.
- [Benchmarking Web Agent Safety under E-commerce Deceptive Interfaces](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13686) — New benchmark shows current web agents are highly susceptible to deceptive UI patterns (fake ads, domain redirects, shopping manipulation), and prompt-based safety constraints frequently fail. Relevant if you're deploying or reviewing any agent code that interacts with external web interfaces.
- [AI is code – and can't be prompted into being smarter](https://www.theregister.com/ai-and-ml/2026/06/14/ai-is-code-and-cant-be-prompted-into-being-smarter/5254141) — The Register argues that prompt engineering has hard ceilings — models will confidently swallow and reproduce whatever you feed them regardless of how cleverly you ask. A useful counterweight to over-relying on prompt tricks as a substitute for model capability or human review.
- [Ponytail – make your AI agent think like the laziest senior dev in the room](https://github.com/DietrichGebert/ponytail) — Small tool/prompting framework designed to bias AI agents toward minimal, low-risk solutions rather than over-engineering — potentially useful for keeping AI-generated code from accruing unnecessary complexity.

## Try This
Run your next LLM-as-a-Judge code review evaluation three times with the candidate answer in alternating positions (first and last), then only act on verdicts that are consistent across all three runs. Based on the arXiv coin-flip paper's findings, this alone will eliminate a large chunk of noise from position bias and run-to-run instability — cheap insurance before you use that verdict to merge or reject AI-generated code.
