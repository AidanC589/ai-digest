General AI Developments

Gemma 4: The best small Multimodal Open Models, dramatically better than Gemma 3 in every way
Google's Gemma 4 launches Apache 2.0 with a 31B dense model tying world-class open models at a fraction of the parameters, day-0 support across llama.cpp, Ollama, and vLLM, and concrete local benchmarks: 162 tok/s on RTX 4090 at 19.5 GB VRAM, 34 tok/s on M4 Mac mini.
Vulnerability Research Is Cooked
Thomas Ptacek argues frontier models are already transforming exploit development into a point-an-agent-at-a-source-tree problem, with Linux kernel and cURL maintainers now receiving 5–10 high-quality, often duplicate AI-generated security reports *per day* — up from 2–3 per week two years ago.

Engineering & AI Workflows

Quoting Kyle Daigle
GitHub commit volume is on pace for 14 billion this year (up from 1 billion total in 2025) and Actions minutes hit 2.1 billion this week alone, the clearest public signal yet of how much AI-assisted development has accelerated output volume.
Using third-party harnesses with your Claude subscriptions
Anthropic is ending subscription coverage for third-party harnesses like OpenClaw effective today; affected users get a one-time credit equal to their monthly plan cost, and must switch to API keys or discounted usage bundles — worth auditing any workflow that pipes Claude through non-Anthropic tooling.
Good Friday
Hermes Agent is emerging as a competing open-source harness to OpenClaw, with developers citing its pluggable memory architecture (Honcho, mem0, RetainDB, and others) and better reliability on long agentic tasks as the differentiator — the lesson being that harness design now matters as much as model choice.
Quoting Willy Tarreau
The HAProxy lead reports AI-generated security reports have gone from noise to signal: most are now correct and duplicate-free enough that they've had to recruit additional maintainers — a direct implication for teams using AI agents in their own security review pipelines.
Taught Claude to talk like a caveman to use 75% less tokens
A system prompt forcing minimal, telegraphic output cuts token usage by ~75%; especially relevant now that subscription harness access is ending and API costs become more visible.

In Claude Code, run /cost after your next non-trivial session — the new v2.1.92 update now breaks down costs per model and shows cache-hit rates separately. Use this to identify which parts of your workflow are cache-missing (burning uncached tokens) and restructure your system prompt or conversation flow to keep stable context at the top where it will be cached on subsequent turns. If you're seeing high uncached token counts on returning sessions, the new footer warning will now tell you exactly how many tokens will go uncached on your next turn, giving you a concrete signal to /clear and restart rather than continuing an expensive stale session.
