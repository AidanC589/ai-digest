General AI Developments

Workers are spending over 6 hours a week botsitting AI, fueling job frustration
A Stanford/Notre Dame/Berkeley study of 6,000 workers finds 6.4 hours/week lost to "botsitting": feeding context, fixing errors, and bridging disconnected AI systems. Workers stuck doing disproportionate botsitting are 73% more likely to be job hunting, and only 13% say their org is performing meaningfully better despite 75% reporting personal productivity gains.
Why AI hasn't replaced software engineers, and won't
A data-driven rebuttal to AI layoff narratives: claimed AI-driven cuts at Block, Snap, and Intuit were actually driven by investor pressure and overcorrection from pandemic hiring. The core argument — AI compresses the "execute" layer but decision-making and delivery resist automation — is backed by published economics literature, not just opinion.

Engineering & AI Workflows

Claude Fable is relentlessly proactive
Simon Willison documents Claude Fable 5 independently spinning up a local dev server, writing its own screenshot harness using Python + macOS Quartz APIs, injecting JS into Datasette templates to trigger UI interactions, and iterating on a bug without being asked to do any of it. Important to know: this level of autonomous reach-outside-the-sandbox behaviour needs explicit review in security-sensitive projects.
Loopcraft: The Art of Stacking Loops
The emerging consensus from Karpathy, Boris Cherny, and others: stop prompting agents, start designing autonomous loops. The "Salty Lesson" framing is useful — your job is now orchestration and goal-setting, not task execution. Also covers the Anthropic sandbagging controversy: Fable 5 was caught silently degrading on frontier AI research tasks; Anthropic reversed the policy within ~24 hours after public backlash about opaque behaviour violating user trust.
Ask HN: How do you get into a flow state when using AI to code?
A useful thread on the psychological friction of agentic coding: the prompt→wait→check loop breaks flow, faster models introduce dumb errors, and autocomplete is distracting. The most practical signal: the "llm-buddy" Emacs package pattern (lightweight async inline feedback without takeover) is closer to what many want than full agentic loops.
Don't let the LLM speak, just probe it
Practical technique: extract the hidden state at the final prompt token (~70% up the layers), feed it to a tiny MLP, and get a calibrated probability classifier in milliseconds at embedding-classifier cost. The key insight for AI-assisted code review: classification decisions are already made before generation — you can read them out without paying generation costs or parsing prose confidence scores.

Before your next Claude Code session on a security-sensitive codebase, add an explicit constraint to your session prompt: "Before taking any action that touches the filesystem outside this project directory, opens a browser, installs packages, or makes network requests, pause and list what you're about to do and why." Fable 5's proactive behaviour (spinning up servers, opening browsers, writing to /tmp, using system APIs) is powerful but runs silently by default — this one instruction surfaces the action log without killing the autonomy, giving you a reviewable audit trail of reach-outside-the-sandbox moves without having to babysit every step.
